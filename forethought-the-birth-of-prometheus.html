Forethought

How a two-hour fire in front of a client became a doctrine, a toolkit, and an agent named Prometheus who makes sure it never happens again.
Eddie Belaval · June 2026 · written from the Donato & Brill install

The install was supposed to take twenty minutes. It took two hours, and the client watched every one of them.

It was the first paid engagement. A law firm, their own server, their own database, their lead partner and her IT contractor both on the call to see the system we built open a real loan file for the first time. The chain worked. It pulled the public records, built the folder, generated the documents, opened the matter exactly the way their people do it by hand, in seconds instead of an afternoon. That part was beautiful.

Then the system tried to write the matter to the database, and the database could not be reached. And we discovered, live, on their time, while they watched, that the host was unreachable from their network. Then that a role had never been created. Then that a password had silently truncated. Then that there were three databases with nearly the same name and we were configured against the wrong one. Each of these was a five minute fix. Together, found one at a time in front of an audience, they were a two hour ordeal. I was sick that day. The whole thing felt like drowning in slow motion.

We got it green. The matter synced. The room was gracious. But I walked away from that call knowing something had gone wrong that had nothing to do with the code.

01The wrong shape

The mistake was not technical. The mistake was that the install was a discovery instead of an execution. Every problem we hit already existed before the call. Every one was findable, alone, the day before. We had turned the shared moment with the client into the place where we found out what was broken, when it should have been the place where we showed them something already proven.

There is a name for the discipline I was fumbling toward. It comes from Palantir and it went mainstream as the AI labs realized the hard part of AI was never the model, it was making it work inside someone else's messy business. A product engineer is one capability for many customers. A forward deployed engineer is one customer, many capabilities. You go inside their walls, you write production code in their live environment, and you own whether it actually works. I had been doing the work for months. I had not yet been doing it with a posture.

02The one rule

The posture turned out to be a single sentence, and everything else falls out of it:

Never debug in front of the client.

The joint call is supposed to be a re-run of something you already ran green in private. If you are diagnosing live, you skipped a step. That is the whole thing. The fix for the two hour install was not a better install script. It was the decision to do the discovery and the breakage where no one is watching, so the shared moment is a demonstration, not a diagnosis.

From that one rule, a structure unfolded over the next two days that I did not plan so much as excavate. It already existed in what we had broken. We just had to write it down.

03The doctrine

First came the lifecycle, the ordered arc of a delivery, each stage with a gate that has to go green before the next one starts.

Release Train → The Cut → Pre-Flight → Install

The Release Train is the sweep before you cut a version: every unfinished job gets a decision, on board or next train, so nothing ships undeclared. The Cut freezes it and publishes the real artifact, because the box installs from the published release, not from a branch, and the two hour install taught us that the hard way. The Pre-Flight is the heart of it: a verify only check you run alone against the real environment until it prints green. That is where you find the unreachable host and the missing role and the truncated password, in private, the day before, so the client never sees them. The Install is the twenty minute demo of something already proven.

Under the lifecycle sat the inversion that made the whole thing possible. The two hour install happened because the boundary of ownership was blurry. So we drew it sharp. The client's IT owns the box, the firewall, the network, and granting access. We own the entire application and data layer, everything that can be tested without their building. When the line is clean, your pre-flight can run solo, because the only thing you need from them ahead of time is access. You never wait on them to debug your layer, and they never wait on you to configure their network.

04The tools, and then the skills

Doctrine that lives only in a document is a wish. So the lifecycle grew hands. A pre-flight harness that connects to the real environment and prints a green and red board, with an error classifier that already knows the five messages that cost us the two hours, each one pre-solved. A release train sweep that inventories every loose job before a cut. A local staging environment, a throwaway copy of the client's stack, where the exact bug that cost two hours can now be reproduced and fixed in seconds, with no one watching and nothing at risk.

Then the tools grew into commands. The runbook stopped being a checklist you follow and became a set of skills you invoke. Seven of them, one per gate. Scaffold a new engagement, sweep the release train, run the pre-flight, scope the gatekeeper, decide custody, check the blind spots, cut over from the old system. The discipline went from something you had to remember while sick to something you could call by name.

05The owner

And then, on the second day, the real turn. Skills are hands. Hands still need someone to decide which one to use and when, someone who carries the doctrine and refuses to let the arc go astray. An agent earns its existence when there is a repeatable arc, deterministic gates, judgment at the seams, and a need to scale. That is exactly what forward deployment had become.

So we built the owner. We named him Prometheus.

The name is the doctrine. Prometheus is forethought. He is the one who thinks ahead, who brings the fire into the human world and owns whether it lights. His brother, the one everyone forgets, is Epimetheus, afterthought, the one who acts first and understands later, who opens the box and discovers the problem only after it is loose in the room. Epimetheus is the two hour install. Epimetheus is debugging in front of the client. Prometheus is the agent we built to make sure we are never him again.

Prometheus owns an engagement end to end and gatekeeps every stage of the arc. He will not let the work advance past a red gate. He runs the breakage in private and brings only green to the shared moment. He carries the field manual as his mind and the seven skills as his hands. And he knows the difference between the decisions that are mechanical, which he simply makes, and the decisions that are human, the money, the custody, the named relationships, the go-live, which he stops and brings to me with a single clean recommendation. Per engagement he takes the client's name. For the law firm, he is Prometheus-Brill.

His first act, the hour he was born, was to audit the very engagement that created him. He walked all thirteen gates and told me, plainly, which were green and which were not yet done. The mechanical spine was sound. But he found three things a green install would have hidden: the contract terms were never papered, the IT contractor had no written escalation path, and the client had never formally acknowledged what done means. None of them were fatal. All of them were exactly the kind of quiet gap that bites a month after a green demo. He found them in an hour, in private, before they could cost anything. Which is the entire point.

06The turn

Here is what actually changed, and it is bigger than a toolkit.

I used to do installs. Now I approve at the gates. One Prometheus owns one install, runs it through the identical disciplined arc, and stops only to bring me the handful of decisions that are genuinely mine. Many of them can run at once. The thing that was a single person drowning in a two hour call has become a practice with a guardian, and my job inside it moved from the chair to the gate. I became the executive producer of a forward deployment practice instead of the operator of one install.

The fire was real. It was a bad day, and I was sick, and the client watched us flail. But the discipline that came out of it is worth more than a smooth first install would have been, because a smooth first install teaches you nothing. The flail is what got written down. The flail is what became the doctrine, and the doctrine is what got hands, and the hands is what got an owner.

We took the fire and gave it forethought. That is the whole story, and it has a name now, and the name stands guard.
