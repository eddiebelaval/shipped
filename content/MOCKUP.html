The Open

There's a thing Anthropic does now that doesn't have a name. They release a flagship model, and in the same breath, they tell you about a better one you can't have. Opus 4.6 in November. Opus 4.7 today. Mythos in between, gated, available only to a handful of security partners.

Call it the shadow release. The model that ships sets the floor. The model that doesn't reveals the ceiling. The space between them is where everyone who builds with this stuff now lives.

Three weeks of velocity below. Models, agents, infrastructure, a security pact with twelve names you'd recognize, twenty-six versions of Claude Code, a tokenizer change you'll feel in your bill. Plus the shadow.

The Lead Story

Opus 4.7, and the chart that wasn't about Opus 4.7

Opus 4.7 went generally available this morning. Same pricing as 4.6 — five dollars per million input, twenty-five per million output. Better at coding, sharper on hard software engineering, with the kind of self-verification that previously required you to paper over with scaffolding. Vision tripled in resolution: 2,576 pixels on the long edge, more than three times what prior versions could see. The model can now read dense screenshots and pixel-perfect references without you down-sampling first.

A new effort level called xhigh slotted in between high and max — finer control on the reasoning-versus-latency dial. Claude Code now defaults to xhigh on every plan. The migration notes mention a tokenizer change: the same input may consume 1.0 to 1.35× more tokens depending on what you feed it. Re-tune your prompts. The bill will tell you which way it broke.

But the headline isn't the model.

The headline is the chart Anthropic published next to the model.

It shows Opus 4.7 beating Opus 4.6. Beating GPT-5.4. Beating Gemini 3.1 Pro across the relevant benchmarks. And then it shows Opus 4.7 losing to a fourth bar labeled Mythos Preview. The fourth bar is taller than every other bar. The fourth bar is the model you can't use.

That's the news. Anthropic shipped a flagship and conceded, in the same breath, that it isn't the frontier.

The frontier is in a vault, working on the OSS-Fuzz corpus, finding 27-year-old vulnerabilities in OpenBSD. Opus 4.7 is the floor of what you can buy this week. Mythos is the ceiling of what exists. The distance between them is the new normal.

Investigation

The Glasswing Bet

How twelve companies committed their critical infrastructure to a model nobody can use — and why Anthropic let them.

by Eddie Belaval

On April 7, twelve organizations — AWS, Apple, Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Cisco, CrowdStrike, JPMorganChase, the Linux Foundation, Palo Alto Networks, Broadcom, and Anthropic — committed to using Mythos Preview to harden critical infrastructure. The model had already been working. What they announced was the governance structure for what it found.

Before Glasswing went public, Mythos had found vulnerabilities in every major operating system and browser. A 27-year-old memory management bug in the OpenBSD kernel. A 16-year-old integer overflow in FFmpeg's codec decoder. A chained privilege escalation in the Linux kernel that moves from user space to ring-0. A browser exploit that escapes both the renderer sandbox and the OS sandbox. These aren't theoretical. They're working exploits that Mythos produced automatically, against production codebases, in hours.

"The threats Mythos found would have taken our teams years to discover manually. What took us months of review took the model an afternoon."

Igor Tsyganskiy, CTO, Microsoft

On the CyberGym benchmark — the industry's most comprehensive evaluation of AI cybersecurity capability — Mythos scores 83.1%. Opus 4.6, the previous public flagship, scored 66.6%. GPT-5.4 scores somewhere in the mid-50s. The gap is not marginal. In the context of offensive security, it is the difference between a capable junior analyst and a senior threat researcher who has read every CVE ever filed and has perfect pattern recall.

For Firefox vulnerabilities specifically: in several hundred attempts, Opus 4.6 produced working JavaScript shell exploits twice. Mythos succeeded 181 times. This is the number that made people take Glasswing seriously.

What Mythos Found
Verified zero-days (pre-disclosure)
OpenBSD kernel 27 years
Memory management bug, 1997-era code. Exploitable remotely without authentication.
FFmpeg codec decoder 16 years
Integer overflow in video stream parsing. Triggers on malformed input in any application using libavcodec.
Linux kernel privilege chain active
Multi-step escalation, user space to ring-0. Chained three separate weaknesses in process scheduling.
Cross-browser sandbox escape active
Escapes renderer sandbox and OS sandbox in a single exploit chain. Reproduced on Chrome and Firefox.

$100M in model credits. $4M in donations to OpenSSF, Apache, and Alpha-Omega. Public progress reports in 90 days. These are the financial terms. The real terms are simpler: twelve organizations agreed to let a model they don't control audit their most critical codebases, and to share what it finds publicly.

This is the first time Anthropic has explicitly held a model back, post-evaluation, on safety grounds that survived peer review. The AISI assessed Mythos's offensive capabilities. The assessment concluded that the capabilities were significant enough to warrant restricted deployment. Anthropic agreed. The model is not on the public API. It is, instead, inside critical infrastructure at twelve of the most security-conscious organizations in the world, doing defensive work under controlled conditions.

"We've been running it against our internal systems for six weeks. The quality of findings is unlike anything we've seen from automated tools."

Jeetu Patel, EVP Security & Collaboration, Cisco

Whether this governance model scales is the open question. The 90-day report will be the first real test. What Anthropic has demonstrated is a willingness to act on capability evaluations — not just publish them. That matters more than the benchmark number.

21 Days, 56 Releases

Claude Code shipped twenty-six versions between v2.1.85 and v2.1.111. This is what velocity looks like from the changelog.

by Eddie Belaval

The thing about twenty-six versions in twenty-one days is that it doesn't feel like twenty-six versions. It feels like a product that isn't done. A product that keeps discovering what it is.

The arc across this window: Claude Code started as a terminal-native coding assistant. It ended as something closer to an IDE with opinions. The sidebar. Multiple sessions side by side. Integrated terminal. File editing. HTML and PDF preview. A faster diff viewer. These aren't version bumps — they're architectural decisions arriving in rapid sequence, each one changing how the tool is meant to be used.

Also Shipped

The agent stack got serious.

Three releases in two days reframed what "build an agent on Claude" means. Managed Agents (April 8, public beta, header managed-agents-2026-04-01) — a fully managed harness with secure sandboxing, built-in tools, and server-sent event streaming. The advisor tool (April 9) — pair a faster executor model with a higher-intelligence advisor that provides strategic guidance mid-generation. Long-horizon agentic workloads get close to advisor-solo quality at executor-model cost. The ant CLI — a command-line client for the Claude API with native Claude Code integration and YAML-based versioning of API resources.

The era of writing your own loop, building your own sandbox, and stitching your own context window is closing. Anthropic is taking that work in-house.

Managed Agents — Minimum Viable Setup
Python beta header: managed-agents-2026-04-01
import anthropic client = anthropic.Anthropic() # Create a managed agent agent = client.managed_agents.create( name="code-reviewer", description="Reviews code for security issues", tools=["code_execution", "file_read"] ) # Run a session session = client.managed_agents.sessions.create( agent_id=agent.id, messages=[{"role": "user", "content": "Review this PR for SQL injection."}] ) # Stream SSE events for event in session.stream(): print(event)

Claude Code grew a body.

Twenty-six versions shipped between v2.1.85 and v2.1.111. The moves that matter: Routines — saved Claude Code configurations with prompts, repos, and connectors that run automatically on Anthropic's cloud, packaged once. A UI redesign — multiple Claude sessions side by side in one window with a sidebar, integrated terminal, file editing, HTML and PDF preview, a faster diff viewer. /ultrareview — comprehensive parallel-analysis code review using cloud compute. /team-onboarding — generates a teammate ramp-up guide from your local usage. Push notification tool. PowerShell rolling out on Windows. Auto mode for Max subscribers.

The CLI is becoming an IDE. The IDE is becoming a control plane.

Two papers worth your Friday.

Automated Alignment Researchers (April 14) — Anthropic using LLMs to scale scalable oversight. Read it with the Mythos announcement in your other tab; the same week they hold a model back for safety, they're publishing on how to use models to do the safety work itself. Emotion Concepts in Large Language Models (March) — internal representations of emotions in Claude that generalize across contexts and shape behavior. The wrong question is whether the models have emotions. The right question is whether they have coherent internal representations of emotions that meaningfully change what they output. Both papers point at the same thing: the lab is trying to do interpretability and oversight at the speed of capability.

Quiet on the Wire

Mythos public release — no date, no signal, no roadmap. The Bedrock Messages API research preview is us-east-1 only; regional rollout unannounced. Sonnet 4 and Opus 4 retire from the API on June 15, 2026 — if you haven't migrated, the clock started two days ago. Sonnet 3.5's 1M context beta dies April 30. The Long-Term Benefit Trust appointed Vas Narasimhan to the Board of Directors on April 14; what that signals about the pharma-and-policy axis of governance is worth watching over the next quarter.

Term of the Issue
shadow release /ˈʃæ.doʊ rɪˈliːs/  noun

A model announced publicly alongside a flagship release that surpasses it in measured capability but remains unavailable on the public API. The flagship sets the floor of what builders can access; the shadow sets the ceiling of what exists. The space between them defines the current state of the frontier.

First used in this issue. Coined to describe Anthropic's practice beginning with the Mythos Preview announcement, April 7, 2026.
