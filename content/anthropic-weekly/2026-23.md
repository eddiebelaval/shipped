# Shipped., Week of 2026-06-01 (Anthropic)

**Cover Line:** The week Anthropic filed its S-1, set a public timeline for Mythos, deprecated Opus 4.1, and shipped six Claude Code builds.

---

## The Open

Monday, June 1. Anthropic submitted a confidential draft registration statement on Form S-1 to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/confidential-draft-s1-sec)) The direction: public markets. The timeline: as early as October 2026. The filing landed less than a week after the company closed a $65 billion round that put its post-money valuation near $965 billion, led by Altimeter, Dragoneer, Greenoaks, and Sequoia. So the IPO is not a fundraising act. The money is already in the bank. The S-1 is a different move: a company that has been allergic to disclosure for its whole life agreeing, on a clock, to open the books.

The rest of the week did not pause for the filing. Thirty-four hours later, Glasswing expanded to approximately 200 organizations across 15-plus countries, with Mythos-class models "in the coming weeks" buried near the bottom of the announcement. Wednesday: a year of AI threat data mapped to MITRE ATT&CK and contributed to Verizon's DBIR, and the Claude Partner Network got formal tiers. Thursday and Friday: four more Claude Code builds, the last two focused squarely on enterprise fleet management. Friday afternoon: TypeScript and Python SDKs coordinated a release with client middleware and Opus 4.1 formally deprecated across every platform. The same day, Anthropic published a research note measuring Opus 4.7 against NMR spectroscopy, and ran the third installment of its developer conference, the first one held outside the United States, live from Tokyo.

There is a thread connecting the product sprint to the filing. The 10,000 high-severity flaws found by Glasswing, the threat analysis mapped to MITRE ATT&CK, the version pinning in Claude Code for enterprise fleets: these are not incidental. They are the evidence the S-1 prospectus will eventually describe. A week that produces both an IPO filing and a chemistry benchmark is a week telling you what kind of company is about to trade publicly.

---

## The Lead Story

The S-1 mechanics are these. Anthropic submitted a confidential draft on Form S-1 to the SEC on June 1, 2026. Confidential status is a procedural choice, not a secrecy flex: it lets a company start the SEC review clock and trade comments with the regulator before any of its numbers become public, then flip the whole document open at least a couple of weeks ahead of the roadshow. Until that flip, the risk factors, the revenue detail, the margins, and the growth trajectory stay private. What is public on day one: the direction and the scale. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/confidential-draft-s1-sec))

What had already leaked into reporting: the annualized revenue run rate has crossed $47 billion. ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/01/anthropic-ipo-s1-prospectus.html), [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/06/01/nx-s1-5843199/anthropic-ipo-filing-ai-large)) That number deserves a frame. Salesforce, which built its enterprise platform over two decades, reported $34.9 billion for all of fiscal 2024. Anthropic's current run rate is 35 percent above that. The velocity: $9 billion at year-end 2025, $14 billion in February, $30 billion in April, $47 billion now. ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/05/29/anthropic-raises-65-billion-at-record-965-billion-valuation-promises-mythos-ai-model-in-wide-release-in-coming-weeks-releases-claude-opus-4-8/)) Four data points in five months. The line is nearly vertical, and a vertical line is exactly the thing a prospectus has to translate into something an underwriter can defend with a straight face.

Then there is the valuation. The $65 billion round that closed the week before the filing set the post-money mark near $965 billion, with Altimeter, Dragoneer, Greenoaks, and Sequoia leading. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/confidential-draft-s1-sec)) That is the number the public-market debut is built on top of. A company does not raise $65 billion privately and then go public to raise more. It goes public to give those backers a clock and a door. The S-1 is the door.

Target listing: as early as October 2026. Wilson Sonsini, the firm that handled Google's 2004 IPO, is reportedly engaged as legal counsel. The choice of counsel is its own tell. Google's 2004 listing is the canonical case of a founder-controlled tech company going public on its own terms, dual-class shares and all, while the bankers grumbled. No share count yet. No price range. Both wait on market conditions and the eventual public prospectus.

What the filing will need to address is the cost of safety, and this is where Anthropic's S-1 will not read like anyone else's. The Responsible Scaling Policy, the alignment research compute, the Glasswing infrastructure, and the interpretability evaluations required to clear Mythos for public release are all real expenditures. None of them appear in the run-rate number. They appear in the cost line. Most frontier-lab pitches treat safety as a brand asset, a thing you say. Anthropic has spent the week making safety a thing you can count: 10,000 critical flaws found, 832 malicious accounts mapped, a model held back until "additional safeguards are finalized." Public markets will want to know how all of that flows through to margins, because a buyer of the stock is, in effect, underwriting the bet that responsible scaling and a vertical revenue line can coexist.

What the filing will not need to justify, at this valuation, is the market's belief in the product. $47 billion in annualized revenue settled that argument. The harder question is the one the prospectus exists to answer: whether a company can grow at this slope while spending real money to slow itself down at the exact moments its rivals speed up. The S-1, when it becomes public, is the document that explains the gap between the safety story and the growth story. It will be the read of the year.

## Also Shipped

**Glasswing expanded, and Mythos got a timeline.** Anthropic added 150 organizations across 15-plus countries to Project Glasswing, bringing total partners to approximately 200. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/expanding-project-glasswing)) New sectors: power, water, healthcare, communications, hardware. Samsung and NATO confirmed as additions, per Financial Times reporting. Partners have collectively found more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity security flaws in widely deployed systems since launch, per [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/02/anthropic-mythos-ai-project-glasswing.html). The access program is not ending. It is becoming the on-ramp to a public product: Mythos-class models will be available to all customers "in the coming weeks," once additional safeguards are ready.

**A year of AI threats, mapped.** Wednesday, Anthropic published an analysis of 832 accounts banned for malicious cyber activity between March 2025 and March 2026, mapped to MITRE ATT&CK. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/AI-enabled-cyber-threats-mitre-attack)) MITRE ATT&CK is the field's shared grammar for describing how an intrusion actually unfolds, stage by stage, and the finding that matters is where on that grid the AI shows up. Not at the front. AI use concentrates at later-stage, higher-complexity attack operations rather than reconnaissance, and the activity is becoming more autonomous over the year. That is the inversion of the comforting story, the one where models only help script-kiddies write phishing emails. The blast radius here is everyone who runs the widely deployed software Glasswing partners are probing, because the same capability that finds 10,000 flaws on defense is the capability being measured climbing the kill chain on offense. Anthropic contributed the analysis to Verizon's 2026 Data Breach Investigations Report, the report security teams actually cite in board decks, placing its own threat data inside the industry's canonical taxonomy. The timing, one day after expanding Glasswing, was not accidental. Defense expansion on Tuesday, the threat receipts on Wednesday: the order is the argument.

**The partner network got tiers.** Wednesday, the Claude Partner Network formalized three service tiers with a public portal. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/news/services-track-partner-hub)) Select: 10 certified practitioners, 2 production deployments. Preferred: 100 practitioners, 15 active customers. Global Premier: 1,000 practitioners, 100 customers across 3-plus regions, executive-backed joint business plan. The Claude Partner Hub gives partners a daily-refreshed view of where they stand. Since March 2026: 40,000-plus firms applied, 10,000-plus consultants certified.

**Claude Code: six builds, one arc.** Six Claude Code versions shipped Tuesday through Friday. The early-week builds tightened security in ways that read like a checklist written by someone who has watched an agent go wrong. v2.1.160 made Claude prompt before writing to shell startup files (`.zshenv`, `.zlogin`, `.bash_login`) and, in acceptEdits mode, before touching build-tool config that grants code execution: `.npmrc`, `.yarnrc`, `bunfig.toml`, `.bazelrc`, `.pre-commit-config.yaml`, the `.devcontainer/` directory. Each of those files is a quiet way to make a machine run code on next login, which is exactly why an autonomous editor should not write them unasked. v2.1.161 turned `OTEL_RESOURCE_ATTRIBUTES` into labels on metric datapoints, so a platform team can finally slice agent telemetry by team and repo, and it redacted MCP credentials that `claude mcp list/get/add` had been printing in the clear. v2.1.162 added `waitingFor` to `claude agents --json`, so automation knows what a blocked session is actually blocked on instead of just that it is stuck. Thursday's v2.1.163 added managed version pinning via `requiredMinimumVersion` and `requiredMaximumVersion`: Claude Code refuses to start outside the allowed range, no override, deployed through `managedSettings.json`. That is the single most enterprise sentence Anthropic shipped all week. It means a security org can guarantee that every machine in a fleet, and every CI runner, is on a version it has actually reviewed. Friday's v2.1.166 added `fallbackModel` configuration, up to three alternatives tried in order when the primary is overloaded, and hardened cross-session messaging so relayed messages no longer carry user authority. ([GitHub](https://github.com/anthropics/claude-code/releases)) The arc across the week: the team is building for fleets, and the people who buy fleets are the people who read S-1s.

**SDK day: middleware and a deprecation.** Friday, Anthropic coordinated a release across all TypeScript platform SDKs and the Python SDK. @anthropic-ai/sdk v0.101.0 adds client middleware to the HTTP stack, mirrored across the Vertex, Foundry, Bedrock, and AWS platform wrappers. ([GitHub TypeScript SDK](https://github.com/anthropics/anthropic-sdk-typescript/releases)) Middleware is the unglamorous primitive every production team eventually wants: a documented seam to inject logging, retries, headers, and tracing without monkey-patching the client. Shipping it across five packages on the same day is the platform equivalent of the version-pinning move, governance for people who run Claude at scale rather than features for people trying it out. Claude Opus 4.1 is formally deprecated across all five packages, which sends operators a clear chore: audit for explicit `claude-opus-4-1` strings before the retirement window closes. Python SDK anthropic v0.106.0 carries the same deprecation plus a schema transform fix (`$defs` preserved when the schema root is a `$ref`) and a Foundry client fix. ([GitHub Python SDK](https://github.com/anthropics/anthropic-sdk-python/releases)) Security disclosures now route to HackerOne, which is a small line with a large meaning the week before an IPO: a formal channel for outside researchers to report holes, on the record.

**Claude sat a chemistry exam.** Friday, Anthropic published "Making Claude a chemist," measuring Opus 4.7 against NMR spectroscopy, the read-the-spectrum-infer-the-structure step a chemist runs before any synthesis begins. ([anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/research/making-claude-a-chemist)) The model matches dedicated NMR software and beats it on some specific tasks, with the evaluation led by Anthropic chemist David Kamber. The scope is deliberately narrow, one analytical workflow step rather than a broad chemistry credential, and the restraint is the point. The question being asked is precisely where a general model starts to displace a bought specialist tool inside a working lab. That is a different sales motion than chat, and it is the kind of concrete, defensible capability claim a company likes to have on the record the same week it tells the SEC it is worth roughly a trillion dollars.

**Service disruption (resolved).** Elevated error rates across Claude AI, Claude Console, Claude API, and Claude Code were reported starting at 2:19 AM ET on June 2. Root cause identified and resolved same day. SQ Magazine tracked 14 user-reported incidents on DownDetector across the window. No public post-mortem at time of this compilation, which is its own small data point for a company about to acquire shareholders who expect them.

## Quiet on the Wire

No new model releases in the window. The big pending shoe is Mythos: general availability "in the coming weeks," gated on safeguards, with the week's own language ("when appears to be June") pointing at a near-term public release rather than a someday. Watch for the safeguard sign-off, because that is the event that converts a vetted-partner program into a product anyone can buy. On the developer-relations side, Anthropic ran Code w/ Claude in Tokyo on June 5 and 6, the third event in the series and the first held outside the United States, with day-one keynotes and breakouts streamed live. A US lab taking its flagship developer conference international is a soft signal about where it thinks the next tranche of enterprise demand lives. API, Claude Apps, and system-prompt release notes documentation pages returned HTTP 403 throughout the week's sweeps; those areas cannot be confirmed for this record. The S-1 is confidential: no follow-on public disclosures from the filing in the same week it was filed.
