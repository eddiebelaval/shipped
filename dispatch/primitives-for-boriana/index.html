01The lens
What a primitive actually is
A primitive is the smallest unit of work that does one job, has a clear contract, and composes cleanly with other units.

That is the whole definition. Three parts. One job. Clear contract. Composes cleanly.

In a kitchen, salt is a primitive. A sharp knife is a primitive. Mise-en-place is a primitive. Nobody confuses a knife with the dish. The chef's skill is not in inventing new tools. It is in composing reliable tools at speed.

In your work, a keyword brief is a primitive. A title-tag rule is a primitive. A schema injection is a primitive. An internal-link audit is a primitive. A rank-tracker pull is a primitive. A GSC export is a primitive. A content brief template is a primitive. A redirect map is a primitive. A canonical decision is a primitive. None of these is "SEO." SEO is what you get when you compose them.

This is where most people get the lens wrong. They think the work is the primitive. The work is the chain. Primitives are cheap. Chains are the deliverable. And almost nobody names theirs, so almost nobody can fix theirs.

We talked about this over the weekend. You said you wanted it written down. The reason I think it lands for you specifically is that SEO is a discipline where the chain shape is already half-visible. You can almost see it when you describe your week. The lens just gives you the words to lock it in place.

02The shape
Ten mechanical stages, one human gate
Every chain in an operation walks through the same shape.
Ten mechanical stages plus one human gate at the end. The mechanical stages can be agents, scripts, templates, checklists, anything. The human gate is the one moment a person still looks at the output and says, "yes, ship." That moment never disappears, no matter how much automation you bolt on. That is not a limitation. That is the design.
01
Trigger named
A ranking drop. A new keyword cluster. A client onboard. A content gap. A competitor's new feature page. Most operators cannot list all their trigger surfaces.
02
Scope fenced
What this chain is, and what it is not. Without a fence, chains bleed into each other and the team works on whatever shouts loudest.
03
Chain sketched
The links written out in order on one page. Keyword cluster, intent, brief, draft, on-page, schema, internal links, QA, staging, publish, measure.
04
Foundation reachable
GSC token works. Ahrefs export is automated. CMS permissions live. The substrate is in place. This is where most "let me automate my workflow" projects die.
05
Primitives built
Each link does one job with a clean contract. The brief primitive returns a brief in standard format. The on-page primitive returns a checked draft. One job, one contract, every time.
06
Integration
The links actually talk to each other. This is where most chains break. The primitives are fine. The seams are duct tape. You are the duct tape.
07
QA
A rubric checks each primitive against its contract before it passes downstream. The rubric is not optional. The rubric is the chain's immune system.
08
Polish
Canonicals where the same content lives twice. Hreflang where the site has multiple regions. Meta length. Open Graph fallbacks. The boring details that separate work that ranks from work that almost ranks.
09
Launch prep
Staging, preview, schema validation, redirects mapped, indexing requested, social cards rendered. The final pass before live, with everything verified.
10
Ship-ready
The chain runs end to end. From trigger to publish, with no person in the seam holding it together. The chain is ready to be pulled.
+1
The Human Gate
A person looks at the output and says, "this goes live." For high-stakes work that gate is heavy. For low-stakes work it is light. But it never disappears. Automation does not mean autopilot. The gate is where judgment lives. Stop and look at the count. Ten of these are mechanical. One is human. Most operators run their week with the inverse ratio. They are doing the ten and skipping the one. That is the trade you want to reverse.
03Six points
The ones I had to hear twice
1
A primitive does one job. If you cannot describe what a step does in one sentence, it is not a primitive yet, it is a confused bundle.
A "content workflow" is not a primitive. A "keyword brief generator that takes a cluster and returns a filled template" is a primitive. The discipline is naming until the noun does one thing.
2
Composition is the work, not primitive-building.
People love building new tools. New scrapers, new dashboards, new prompts. The yield is in composing the tools you already have into reliable chains. Most operations have a hundred half-built primitives nobody chained together. The 50x is in chaining, not in building.
3
The waste lives in the glue, and you are the glue.
Look at any one of your chains and ask where you are inserting yourself by hand. Copying GSC data into a sheet. Reformatting a brief for a writer. Pulling rank data into a client report. That is glue work. You are being a primitive when you should be the strategist composing the primitives. It is the most expensive failure mode in any operation because your time is the most expensive input.
4
Half-finished chains look like systems and bleed money.
The chain that mostly works, except for that one step where you have to log in and click the thing, is not a system. It is a chain with a human-shaped hole in it. Those holes are where errors live, deadlines miss, and burnout grows. A 90 percent automated chain with a hole is worse than a 60 percent automated chain that runs clean to the gate.
5
Naming is half the value.
The moment you give a chain a name, you can talk about it, fix it, hand it off, document it, and measure it. Until then it lives in your head and dies when you sleep. Most of the chains you run every week do not have names yet. Give them names this week and the rest of the work starts.
6
The human gate stays. That is not a flaw, that is the point.
Ten mechanical stages get the work to ship-ready. The one human gate is where you, the operator, say "yes, this is good, this is on-brand, this is correct, this goes live." That is judgment work. That is what you are paid for. The whole point of building the chain is so you get to spend your day at the gate, not stuck inside stages four through nine doing copy-paste.
04The assignment
Read this part. Then do it.
Before Friday, do five things.
01
Walk your week and list every chain that fired. Pull your calendar, your inbox, your task list, your last seven days of Slack. For each piece of work, ask: what was the trigger, what was the chain, what was the output. Do not stop at the obvious ones. The status report you sent Monday is a chain. The keyword research you did for the new client is a chain. Get them all on one page. Plain text, no fancy tool. The point is to see the graph.
02
Mark each step in each chain with one of three colors. Green for automated, the step happens without you touching it. Yellow for glued, you are personally moving data or formatting or copying. Red for missing, the step should exist but does not, and you are catching it in your head every time. You will have more yellow than you expected and probably more red than you want to admit. Good. That is the diagnostic.
03
Pick one chain. Just one. The one with the most yellow, ideally one you run weekly, ideally one you would still be running a year from now. That is the chain to finish first. Not because it is the most glamorous. Because it compounds. A weekly chain that runs clean for a year is fifty repetitions of leverage.
04
Name every primitive in that one chain. Each step gets a noun. "GSC top-queries pull." "Cluster classifier." "Brief generator." "Draft writer." "On-page checker." "Internal-link suggester." "Publish gate." Each noun does one job. If you cannot name a step in one noun, it is two steps wearing one coat. Split it.
05
Find the glue and replace it. Run the chain end to end once, with the human gate intact. No skipping the gate. You still look at the output before it ships. But you are now the person at the gate, not the person inside the chain. Feel the difference. Time how long it took before, time how long it takes now. Write down what you did with the time you got back.
The line that started this
Every operation is a graph of triggers wired to primitives. Most operators have unnamed primitives, half-composed chains, and humans doing primitive work. The job is to name them, compose them, and free the humans to do the parts that need judgment.

That is SEO. That is law firm ops. That is a restaurant kitchen. That is a TV production. The lens is universal because operations are universal. The discipline is naming until the chain runs.

You already do most of this for clients without calling it that. The only thing you needed was the word for it. Now you have the word. Use it on yourself first.

Tell me which chain you picked when you've done it.
